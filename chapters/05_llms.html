    Chapter 05 · One Model, Many Tasks

    Large Language Models: Exponential AI's universal language engine

    Scale a Transformer, pretrain it on much of the written world, and something remarkable emerges: a single model that drafts replies, summarises contracts, writes product copy, and answers questions — without task-specific training. This chapter is where Exponential AI stops building one model per problem and starts building on one model for everything.

    ~18 min read Level 1 → Level 3 Mermaid + ASCII diagrams Quiz included
    01 · Recap & Introduction

    Where Exponential AI left off

    Chapter 4 gave Exponential AI the Transformer: an architecture that reads all tokens in parallel and uses self-attention to learn which words matter to which. That solved the RNN's twin flaws — slowness and forgetfulness — and made it possible to train on enormous text corpora. But an architecture is only a blueprint. The question this chapter answers is: what happens when you train that blueprint at unprecedented scale?

    The answer is the Large Language Model. Pretrain a big Transformer to predict the next token across trillions of words, and it absorbs grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, and style — becoming a general-purpose language engine. Exponential AI retires a zoo of narrow NLP models (a classifier here, a summariser there) and replaces them with one foundation model it can steer by instruction. This is the moment AI becomes a platform.

    What you'll be able to do after this chapter

    Explain next-token prediction, tokenisation, and context windows; distinguish pretraining, fine-tuning, and RLHF; reason about parameters, emergence, and hallucination; and see why controlling an LLM's input becomes the discipline of Chapter 6.

    02 · Why this technology exists

    Why the world invented LLMs

    LLMs exist because of a surprising empirical discovery: scale unlocks generality. Train a Transformer on one narrow task and you get a narrow tool. Train a much larger one to simply predict the next token over a vast, diverse corpus, and it implicitly learns thousands of tasks at once — translation, summarisation, question answering, coding — because all of them are expressible as "continue this text sensibly."

    This collapses the old economics. Previously Exponential AI needed a labelled dataset and a bespoke model for every language task. An LLM is pretrained once (at great expense, usually by a vendor) and then reused across every task via instructions, with little or no extra training. The marginal cost of a new capability drops from "months of ML work" to "write a good prompt" — which is precisely why the whole enterprise reorganised around it.

    There is a subtler reason too, and it is the one that reshapes org charts. Because an LLM's interface is natural language, the people who define its behaviour no longer have to be machine-learning engineers. A support lead can describe the tone and policy for refund replies; a merchandiser can specify how product copy should read. The model becomes a shared capability that many teams steer directly, and the ML team's role shifts from building a model per problem to operating one platform reliably — grounding it, evaluating it, and guarding it. Nearly every remaining chapter is really about that operational discipline.

    03 · Problem statement

    The problem, stated precisely

    The training objective is deceptively simple: given a sequence of tokens, predict the next one. Formally, maximise the probability the model assigns to real continuations across the corpus. Do this over a large enough model and dataset, and the weights come to encode a compressed, queryable model of language and much of the knowledge expressed in it.

    But simplicity at training time creates hard problems at use time. The model has no database — it generates plausible text, which means it can produce confident falsehoods (hallucination). It only sees what fits in its context window. And its knowledge is frozen at training time. Every later chapter — prompting, RAG, guardrails, context engineering — is a response to one of these three constraints.

    Three levels of understanding

    One idea, three depths

    Level 1 · Simple

    A supercharged autocomplete

    An LLM predicts the next word, over and over, extremely well. Because it read so much, its guesses are coherent, knowledgeable, and useful — but they are still guesses, not lookups.

    Level 2 · Engineering

    Pretrain, fine-tune, align

    Pretraining learns language from raw text; supervised fine-tuning teaches it to follow instructions; RLHF aligns it with human preferences. Inference then samples tokens autoregressively from the resulting distribution.

    Level 3 · Architect

    A probabilistic capability, not an oracle

    Treat the LLM as a stochastic reasoning engine with a fixed context budget and no ground truth. Architecture means surrounding it with retrieval, validation, and guardrails so its fluency becomes reliable.

    04 · Historical evolution

    From word vectors to foundation models

    The road from "computers handle words as symbols" to "one model does everything with language" was a decade of steadily larger models on steadily more text.

    2013 — Word embeddings

    word2vec/GloVe turn words into vectors where meaning is geometry. Useful, but no sentence-level understanding.

    2018 — Pretrained Transformers (BERT/GPT)

    Pretrain on huge corpora, fine-tune per task. NLP is transformed almost overnight.

    2020 — Scale & few-shot (GPT-3)

    175B parameters; tasks solved from instructions and examples alone, no fine-tuning. "Prompting" is born.

    2022 — Instruction tuning & RLHF

    Aligned chat models make LLMs usable by non-experts. Enterprise adoption explodes.

    2023+ — Multimodal & long-context

    Models take images and huge documents; the platform era of Chapters 7–20 begins.

    05 · Internal architecture

    What's inside an LLM

    An LLM is a deep stack of Transformer blocks (Chapter 4) with an enormous number of parameters — the learned weights, ranging from billions to trillions. Text enters as tokens (sub-word chunks), each mapped to an embedding vector; positional information is added; the stack of attention + feed-forward blocks transforms them; and a final layer produces a probability distribution over the next token.

    Three architectural families exist. Decoder-only models (the GPT family) generate text and dominate today's chat and agent use cases. Encoder-only models (BERT) produce representations for classification and search. Encoder- decoder models (T5) suit translation and summarisation. Exponential AI standardises on a decoder-only chat model for generation, and uses a small encoder model to create the embeddings that will power retrieval in Chapter 7.

    Two numbers dominate how an LLM behaves in practice. The first is its parameter count, which loosely tracks capability and cost: larger models reason better but are slower and pricier per token, so Exponential AI routes easy tasks to a small model and hard ones to a large model — a pattern that pays off directly in Chapter 16. The second is the context window, the number of tokens the model can hold in working memory at once. Everything you want the model to consider — instructions, conversation history, retrieved documents — must fit inside it, and because attention cost grows quadratically with length (Chapter 4), that window is both finite and expensive. These two dials, capability-versus-cost and how-much-it-can-see, frame nearly every design decision in the chapters ahead.

    06 · Step-by-step execution

    How an LLM answers a Exponential AI support question

    1. Tokenise. "Where is my order?" becomes a list of token IDs.
    2. Embed + position. Each token becomes a vector carrying meaning and position.
    3. Attention stack. Every layer lets tokens attend to one another, building contextual meaning.
    4. Predict next token. The final layer outputs a probability for every possible next token.
    5. Sample. Pick a token (greedy or with temperature), append it, and repeat from step 2.
    6. Stop. Continue autoregressively until an end token or length limit — the full reply emerges token by token.

    The autoregressive generation loop

    def generate(prompt, max_tokens=256, temperature=0.7):
    tokens = tokenize(prompt)
    for _ in range(max_tokens):
        logits = model(tokens)          # distribution over next token
        probs  = softmax(logits[-1] / temperature)
        nxt    = sample(probs)          # greedy if temperature -> 0
        tokens.append(nxt)
        if nxt == EOS:
            break
    return detokenize(tokens)
    07 · Analogies

    Four ways to picture an LLM

    An extraordinarily well-read improviser who has absorbed millions of books and can continue any passage convincingly — but who is recalling the gist, not quoting a reference, so it sometimes confabulates.

    A brilliant new hire on day one: fluent, capable, and widely knowledgeable — but with zero access to Exponential AI's private systems and last night's inventory. Onboarding it is Chapters 6–7.

    A stochastic function str -> str with no side effects and no memory between calls. Same input, usually similar output; determinism requires pinning the sampling settings.

    A CPU that only understands one instruction — "predict next token" — but is Turing-rich enough, given the right context in its registers (the prompt), to perform almost any language task.

    11 & 12 · Advantages & disadvantages

    Strengths and costs

    Advantages

    • General-purpose — one model, countless tasks.
    • Fast to deploy — new capabilities via prompts, not projects.
    • Strong language & reasoning out of the box.
    • Composable — the substrate for RAG and agents.

    Disadvantages

    • Hallucination — confident, fluent falsehoods.
    • Frozen knowledge — unaware of anything after training.
    • Context limits — can't see beyond its window.
    • Cost & latency scale with tokens (Ch 16).
    13 & 14 · Best practices & anti-patterns

    Building on LLMs — well and badly

    Best practices

    • Ground with retrieval for anything factual or private (Ch 7).
    • Prefer prompting before fine-tuning; fine-tune only when justified.
    • Constrain outputs (schemas, validation) and verify critical facts.
    • Track token cost & latency from day one.

    Anti-patterns

    • Treating the LLM as a database of facts.
    • Fine-tuning to add knowledge that retrieval would supply better.
    • Trusting unverified output in high-stakes flows.
    • Ignoring prompt injection and other new attack surfaces (Ch 18).

    16 · Architect notes

    Think like an architect

    Trade-off analysis

    An LLM trades guaranteed correctness for extraordinary breadth and flexibility. The architect's job is to buy back correctness selectively — grounding, constraints, human review — where the cost of a wrong answer is high, and to let the model run free where it is low.

    Failure scenario

    Exponential AI lets an ungrounded LLM answer "what's my order status?" It invents a plausible tracking number. The fix isn't a bigger model — it's connecting the model to the order system, which is exactly what RAG (Ch 7) and tools (Ch 8) provide.

    17 · Common mistakes

    Where teams go wrong

    • Assuming the model knows your business — it doesn't; it needs your data supplied.
    • Confusing fluency with accuracy — confident tone is not evidence.
    • Fine-tuning first when a prompt or retrieval would have solved it.
    • Ignoring non-determinism in tests and audits.

    19 · Checklist

    Before building on an LLM

    • Decided prompting vs retrieval vs fine-tuning deliberately.
    • Grounding plan for factual/private answers.
    • Output format constrained and validated.
    • Token cost and latency budgeted (Ch 16).
    • Hallucination and injection risks assessed (Ch 18).
    • Evaluation set defined to measure quality (Ch 17).

    21 · Summary

    Summary

    A Large Language Model is a Transformer scaled until generality emerges. Trained only to predict the next token over a vast corpus, it becomes a single engine for translation, summarisation, drafting, and reasoning — steerable by instruction rather than retraining. For Exponential AI this replaced a fleet of narrow models with one platform, collapsing the cost of every new language capability.

    The power comes with three defining constraints: it hallucinates, its knowledge is frozen, and it can only see what fits in its context window. The rest of this handbook is a systematic campaign against those constraints — and it begins with the cheapest lever of all: how you talk to the model.

    22 · What happens next

    Transition to Chapter 6

    Exponential AI now has a universal language engine — but the same model gives a brilliant answer to one phrasing and a useless one to another. Its behaviour is astonishingly sensitive to its input. If the input is the only thing you fully control, then crafting that input becomes an engineering discipline. That discipline is Chapter 6 — Prompt Engineering, where Exponential AI learns to steer the model reliably before it ever touches its private data.
