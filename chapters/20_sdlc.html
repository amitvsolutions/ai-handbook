    Chapter 20 · The Operational Act Begins

    The AI Development Lifecycle: running AI as a discipline

    AI-native engineering (Ch 19) changed how Exponential AI's engineers build. But a pile of AI features is not a repeatable practice. The AI development lifecycle is the methodology umbrella — how you frame a problem, get the data, experiment, and let evaluation drive iteration — and, above all, how this differs from the classic, spec-driven software SDLC.

    ~18 min read Level 1 → Level 3 Mermaid + ASCII diagrams Quiz included
    01 · Recap & Introduction

    Where Exponential AI left off

    Chapter 19 changed how Exponential AI's engineers build: AI became a first-class teammate that drafts, reviews, and tests code, and the human role shifted toward specification and judgment. That answered "how do individuals work," but not "how does the organisation run an AI initiative from idea to reliable product." A forecasting model, a product-image classifier, and a returns agent were each built by a different team with a different definition of "done." Without a shared discipline, quality drifted and lessons weren't reused.

    The AI development lifecycle (AI SDLC) is that shared discipline: the methodology that governs how AI work is framed, resourced, experimented on, evaluated, and iterated. Its single most important lesson is that building learned, probabilistic systems is fundamentally different from building deterministic software — it is data-centric, experimental, non-deterministic, and evaluation-driven rather than spec-driven. Get that mental shift wrong and every downstream stage inherits the mistake.

    What this chapter owns — and what it hands off

    This chapter is the methodology umbrella: problem framing, data, experimentation, evaluation-driven iteration, and roles. It deliberately does not cover the mechanics of shipping models and prompts — CI/CD, registries, canary/blue-green, rollback — that is Chapter 21 · AIOps. Nor does it cover operating live systems — observability, SLOs, incidents, drift — that is Chapter 22 · AI-SRE. Here we treat deployment and operations only as stages inside the lifecycle, not as mechanics.

    What you'll be able to do after this chapter

    Frame an AI problem and pick the right approach (rules vs ML vs LLM); design a data and experimentation plan; build an evaluation-driven iteration loop; define "good enough to ship" for a probabilistic system; and explain exactly how the AI lifecycle differs from the traditional software SDLC.

    02 · Why this technology exists

    Why AI needs its own lifecycle

    The classic software development lifecycle assumes you can specify correct behaviour in advance: gather requirements, design, implement to the spec, test against it, ship, and maintain. The specification is the source of truth, and a passing test suite means the system is correct. AI breaks that assumption at the root. You cannot write a spec that says "classify this product photo correctly" as a set of rules; the behaviour is learned from data and is only ever correct on average, with a distribution of errors you can measure but not eliminate. The artefact you ship is not code that implements a spec — it is a model (or a prompt-plus-model system) that approximates a target you can only define through examples.

    That single difference cascades. Because behaviour comes from data, data quality and representativeness become the primary lever — often more than model choice. Because results are probabilistic, "does it work?" becomes "how well, on which slices, at what confidence?" — a measurement question, not a yes/no. Because you cannot reason your way to the answer, progress is experimental: you form hypotheses, run trials, and keep what the numbers reward. And because the world drifts and the target is fuzzy, the system is never truly finished — it is continuously re-evaluated and improved. A lifecycle that ignores these realities (treating a model like a feature you build once and mark done) produces demos that impress and production systems that quietly rot.

    A third reason the discipline exists is coordination. An AI initiative pulls together product managers, domain experts, data engineers, applied scientists, ML and software engineers, and — later — the platform and reliability teams. Each has a different notion of success and a different rhythm. The lifecycle exists to give them one shared spine: a common way to frame the problem, agree on evaluation, and decide when something is ready to move from notebook to product. Without it, Exponential AI relearns the same lessons on every project.

    03 · Problem statement

    The problem, stated precisely

    Given a business goal that is fuzzy ("reduce refund-handling time"), data that is imperfect, and a solution space that is probabilistic, define a repeatable process that turns the goal into a well-scoped AI problem, produces evidence about whether a solution is good enough, and keeps that solution good as the world changes — all while coordinating many roles and managing the risk that a non-deterministic system will behave unexpectedly.

    The organising principle is evaluation drives everything. In spec-driven software the spec is the anchor; in AI the evaluation set and metrics are the anchor. Before building, you define how you will measure success on realistic data, including the slices that matter (new customers, rare product categories, non-English queries). Every experiment is judged against that harness (Ch 17), every iteration is a hypothesis tested by it, and "ready to ship" is a threshold on it. If you cannot measure it, you cannot manage it — and you almost certainly cannot ship it responsibly.

    Three levels of understanding

    One idea, three depths

    Level 1 · Simple

    Cooking, not assembling

    Building software is like assembling furniture from instructions. Building AI is like developing a recipe: you taste, adjust, and taste again. The tasting — evaluation — is the whole job.

    Level 2 · Engineering

    Data + experiments + evals

    Curate representative data, run experiments against a fixed evaluation set, track results, and iterate. Ship when the metric clears a threshold on the slices that matter — not when a checklist is done.

    Level 3 · Architect

    A measurable, continuous loop

    Design the lifecycle so evaluation is the source of truth from framing to production, data and models are versioned, and monitoring feeds the next iteration. The system is never "done," only currently good enough.

    04 · Historical evolution

    From waterfall to eval-driven AI

    The way teams organise software work evolved toward AI's needs in recognisable steps.

    Waterfall SDLC

    Specify, build, test, ship. Works when behaviour can be fully specified in advance.

    Agile / iterative

    Short cycles and feedback accept that requirements change — but the artefact is still deterministic code.

    CRISP-DM & data science

    Explicit data-understanding and modelling phases acknowledge that data, not just code, drives outcomes.

    MLOps & eval-driven LLM development

    Data/model versioning, experiment tracking, and evaluation harnesses make the probabilistic lifecycle repeatable and continuous.

    05 · Internal architecture

    Inside the lifecycle

    The AI lifecycle has recognisable phases, but they form a loop, not a line. Framing: translate a business goal into an AI problem, decide feasibility, and define success metrics and their acceptable thresholds. Data: source, clean, label, and split data; build the evaluation set first and guard it against leakage. Experimentation: try approaches — a rule baseline, a fine-tuned model, a prompted LLM, a RAG system — each measured against the harness and logged so results are reproducible. Evaluation: judge candidates on aggregate metrics and on the slices that matter, plus qualitative review and red-teaming (Ch 17–18).

    Then the lifecycle hands off to the next acts and receives their feedback. Deployment is a stage here — "get the chosen candidate into production safely" — but its mechanics (packaging, CI/CD, canary, rollback) belong to AIOps (Ch 21). Operation is likewise a stage — "keep it healthy" — but observability, SLOs, incidents, and drift detection belong to AI-SRE (Ch 22). What the SDLC owns is the connective tissue: versioned data and models so any result is reproducible; an experiment registry so knowledge compounds; a definition of done expressed as evaluation thresholds; and a feedback path so production signals (quality regressions, new failure modes, incidents) re-enter framing as the next hypothesis. The architecture is a governed loop whose heartbeat is measurement.

    06 · Step-by-step execution

    Exponential AI builds a refund-triage model the disciplined way

    1. Frame. Goal: cut refund-handling time. AI problem: triage incoming refund requests into auto-approve / auto-deny / human-review. Success: ≥95% precision on auto-decisions, measured per product category.
    2. Build the eval set first. Sample 5,000 historical requests, label the correct outcome, and freeze a held-out test set (guarded against leakage) before any modelling.
    3. Baseline. Start with a simple rules baseline so every later experiment has something to beat.
    4. Experiment. Try a prompted LLM, a RAG variant grounded in policy, and a fine-tuned classifier — each scored on the same frozen set and logged with its config.
    5. Evaluate on slices. Check not just overall precision but performance on rare categories and new customers; inspect failures qualitatively.
    6. Decide. The RAG variant clears the threshold overall and on key slices → it is the ship candidate. (How it ships safely is Chapter 21's job.)
    7. Close the loop. Production feedback and incidents (Chapter 22's job to detect) return here as the next iteration's hypotheses.

    A tiny evaluation-driven iteration harness

    EVAL = load_frozen_eval_set("refund_triage_v3")   # versioned, leakage-guarded

def evaluate(candidate):
    preds = [candidate(x) for x in EVAL.inputs]
    return {
        "precision": precision(preds, EVAL.labels),
        "by_slice": precision_by_slice(preds, EVAL),   # rare cats, new custs
    }

def promote_if_ready(candidate, bar=0.95):
    m = evaluate(candidate)
    ok = m["precision"] >= bar and min(m["by_slice"].values()) >= bar - 0.05
    log_experiment(candidate.config, m)               # knowledge compounds
    return "ship-candidate" if ok else "iterate"
    07 · Analogies

    Four ways to picture the AI lifecycle

    Developing a recipe for a restaurant. You don't write the dish once; you cook, taste against a standard, tweak the ingredients (data) and technique (model), and re-taste until it reliably pleases — then keep checking as suppliers change.

    Pharmaceutical R&D: hypotheses, trials, and pre-registered endpoints. You cannot argue a drug into working; you measure it against a protocol — exactly how AI is measured against an evaluation set.

    Test-driven development, but the "tests" are statistical: you write the evaluation set first, then iterate until the system passes to a threshold rather than a boolean pass/fail.

    A control loop with a setpoint: the evaluation metric is the setpoint, experiments are the actuator, and production monitoring is the sensor that feeds the next correction.

    11 & 12 · Advantages & disadvantages

    Adopting a disciplined lifecycle: benefits and costs

    Advantages

    • Reproducible results via versioned data/models.
    • Comparable experiments; knowledge compounds.
    • Objective ship decisions tied to evaluation.
    • Resilient to drift via the feedback loop.

    Disadvantages / realities

    • Upfront cost of building good eval sets.
    • Cultural shift from "done" to "good enough now."
    • Data burden — labelling and governance are real work.
    • Never finished — ongoing investment required.
    13 & 14 · Best practices & anti-patterns

    The lifecycle done well — and badly

    Best practices

    • Write the eval set before the model; guard it against leakage.
    • Always keep a baseline so gains are measurable.
    • Version data and models; log every experiment.
    • Evaluate on slices, not just aggregate metrics.
    • Define "good enough" as a threshold agreed with stakeholders.

    Anti-patterns

    • Demo-driven development — impressive once, unmeasured.
    • Chasing model choice while ignoring data quality.
    • Evaluating on training data (leakage) — inflated scores.
    • "Ship it, it's done" with no monitoring plan.

    Questions you should be able to answer

    It is data-centric, experimental, non-deterministic, and evaluation-driven rather than spec-driven. The evaluation set replaces the spec as the source of truth, data is the primary lever, "done" is a metric threshold, and the system is continuously re-evaluated rather than finished.

    Because it is the anchor for every decision. Defining success on realistic, leakage-free data before modelling prevents you from moving the goalposts to fit a favourite model and makes ship decisions objective.

    Data distributions drift and targets evolve, so a model that clears the bar today can degrade silently. The lifecycle keeps a feedback loop from production back to framing, so the system is continuously re-evaluated and improved.

    As stages within the lifecycle. The SDLC decides what to ship and when it's good enough; the mechanics of shipping are AIOps (Ch 21) and of running it are AI-SRE (Ch 22).

    16 · Architect notes

    Think like an architect

    Trade-off analysis

    Invest disproportionately in evaluation infrastructure. A great model with a weak eval set is unshippable (you can't prove it's safe); a mediocre model with a rigorous eval set is manageable (you know exactly where it fails). Evaluation is the highest-leverage artefact in the entire lifecycle — treat it as a first-class product, versioned and owned.

    Failure scenario

    A demand-forecasting model is validated on data that accidentally includes future information (leakage). It scores brilliantly, ships, and collapses in production. A frozen, leakage-guarded eval set built during framing would have caught the fantasy before launch.

    17 · Common mistakes

    Where teams go wrong

    • No baseline, so nobody can tell if the model actually helps.
    • Optimising the wrong metric (aggregate accuracy while a key slice fails).
    • Confusing a demo with a product — no eval, no monitoring plan.
    • Blurring the boundaries — trying to solve deployment and operations here instead of framing the discipline.

    19 · Checklist

    Before an AI project leaves the methodology stage

    • Business goal translated into a scoped AI problem with metrics.
    • Frozen, representative, leakage-guarded evaluation set exists.
    • A baseline is in place for comparison.
    • Experiments are logged; data and models are versioned.

    21 · Summary

    Summary

    The AI development lifecycle is the methodology umbrella over Exponential AI's AI work. Its defining insight is that building learned systems is data-centric, experimental, non-deterministic, and evaluation-driven — the opposite of the spec-driven software SDLC. The evaluation set replaces the specification as the source of truth; data is the primary lever; "done" becomes a metric threshold checked on the slices that matter; and the system is never finished, only continuously re-evaluated as the world drifts. Deployment and operations appear here only as stages — the methodology decides what to ship and when it is good enough.

    That decision now needs mechanics. Once a candidate clears the lifecycle's exit gate, how do you actually package it, version it, and get it into production safely — with the ability to roll back the moment something looks wrong? That is the build-and-ship act.
