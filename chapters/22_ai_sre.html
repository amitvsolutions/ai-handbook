    AI-SRE: running AI in production

    AIOps (Ch 21) got the new version safely to go-live and left a rollback switch in reach. Keeping it healthy afterwards is a different discipline: observability across quality and cost, SLOs and error budgets, alerting, incident response, on-call, and detecting the silent drift that degrades AI systems without ever throwing an error.

    01 · Recap & Introduction

    Where Exponential AI left off

    Chapter 21 shipped the refund-triage system to production: versioned in a registry, gated on evaluation, rolled out through a canary, and left with a warm rollback. AIOps ends the moment traffic is flowing steadily. But go-live is a beginning, not an end. A model that passed every offline gate can still degrade in the wild as the world shifts, as traffic mixes change, as an upstream dependency wobbles, or as a rare failure mode finally surfaces at scale. Someone has to run the system — watch it, define what "healthy" means, get paged when it isn't, and fix it under pressure.

    AI-SRE is that discipline: site reliability engineering adapted for non-deterministic, learned systems. It extends classic SRE — SLIs, SLOs, error budgets, alerting, on-call, incident response, disaster recovery — with the signals AI uniquely needs: quality and cost as first-class reliability dimensions, not just latency and uptime. A refund agent that is fast, cheap, and up but quietly approving the wrong refunds is unreliable, and only AI-aware operations will notice.

    What this chapter owns — and what it hands off

    This chapter owns operating live AI: observability (traces/logs/metrics plus quality and cost signals), debugging non-deterministic failures, SLIs/SLOs/error budgets, alerting, incident response and runbooks, on-call, drift and silent-degradation detection, and disaster recovery/failover. It does not cover building or shipping — that is Chapter 21 · AIOps — nor the overall methodology — that is Chapter 20 · AI SDLC. When operations reveal a systemic quality problem, the fix re-enters the lifecycle; AI-SRE detects and contains, it doesn't re-run the methodology or re-ship.

    02 · Why this technology exists

    Why running AI needs its own reliability discipline

    Classic SRE was built for deterministic systems whose failures are loud: a service crashes, returns 500s, or gets slow, and metrics like uptime and latency capture health well. AI systems fail differently. The most dangerous failure is silent: the service is up, fast, and cheap, but its answers get worse. A forecasting model slowly mis-predicts as buying patterns shift; a product-image classifier degrades when a new product line photographs differently; a refund agent starts approving edge cases it should escalate. No exception is thrown, so uptime dashboards stay green while quality quietly collapses. AI-SRE exists because you cannot operate such systems with reliability signals that only measure whether the lights are on.

    It also exists because AI failures are non-deterministic and hard to reproduce. The same input can yield different outputs across runs, and a failure may depend on a particular context, retrieval result, or model sampling. Debugging by "run it again and watch" often doesn't work, so AI-SRE leans on tracing — capturing the full context, prompts, retrieved documents, tool calls, and outputs of each request — so a failure can be examined after the fact even if it can't be re-triggered on demand.

    A third driver is cost as a reliability concern. Because inference is billed per token (Ch 16), a subtle regression — a prompt that grows, a retrieval that returns more context, a retry loop that fires too often — can multiply spend without any functional error. An AI system that is correct but suddenly costs 5× is an operational incident. AI-SRE therefore treats cost, alongside quality and latency, as a signal with its own SLOs and alerts.

    03 · Problem statement

    The problem, stated precisely

    Given a live AI system whose failures may be silent, non-deterministic, and expensive rather than loud and reproducible, design operations that (a) make its true health observable across latency, quality, and cost; (b) define what "reliable enough" means and alert when it isn't; (c) enable debugging of failures that can't be reproduced on demand; and (d) detect gradual drift before it harms customers — all while keeping humans sustainably on-call.

    The organising principle is reliability includes quality and cost, not just uptime. Every practice — the signals you collect, the SLOs you set, the alerts you fire, the runbooks you write — must treat "the answers are still good and still affordable" as core to reliability. An AI-SRE practice that inherits classic SRE unchanged will proudly report four-nines availability on a system that is steadily making worse and more expensive decisions.

    Level 2 · Engineering

    Level 3 · Architect

    Design observability so unreproducible failures are debuggable from traces, drift is caught early, cost is bounded, and recovery (rollback/failover) is fast — a system that stays trustworthy in the wild.

    05 · Internal architecture

    Inside an AI-SRE practice

    The foundation is observability with three classic pillars plus two AI ones. Traces capture the full path of a request — the prompt, retrieved context, tool calls, model version, and output — so an unreproducible failure can still be inspected. Logs record events; metrics aggregate them. On top, quality signals (automated evals on sampled live traffic, LLM-as-judge scores, user feedback, escalation rates) and cost signals (tokens per request, spend per outcome) turn "are the answers good and affordable?" into measurable telemetry.

    On these signals sit SLIs/SLOs/error budgets that explicitly include quality and cost, not just latency and availability. Alerting fires when an SLO is at risk; on-call and incident response with runbooks turn a page into a bounded, repeatable response. Drift detection watches input distributions and output quality over time to catch silent degradation early. Disaster recovery and failover keep the system serving when a model provider, retrieval store, or region fails — degrading gracefully (e.g., to a cheaper model or a cached/rule-based fallback) rather than going dark. Crucially, the architecture consumes the health signals that AIOps emitted and can trigger a rollback (AIOps' mechanism) or route a systemic problem back into the SDLC — but it does not build, package, or re-ship; those remain the previous acts' jobs.

    06 · Step-by-step execution

    Exponential AI handles a silent quality regression

    1. Signal. The quality SLI (sampled-eval precision on refund decisions) drifts from 96% toward 92% over two days; cost per decision also creeps up.
    2. Alert. The error budget burn-rate alert fires before the SLO is fully breached; on-call is paged.
    3. Triage from traces. The responder pulls traces for recent low-quality decisions and finds a new product category with photos the retrieval step mis-grounds.
    4. Mitigate. Per the runbook, they trigger a rollback to the previous known-good version (AIOps' switch) to stop the bleeding.
    5. Contain. Quality returns to baseline; the incident is stabilised without a full outage.
    6. Feed back. The root cause — an unseen data slice — becomes a new hypothesis in the SDLC (Ch 20): expand the eval set and retrain/re-prompt. AI-SRE detected and contained; the lifecycle fixes.
    7. Learn. A blameless postmortem updates the runbook and adds a drift check for new product categories.

    A quality + cost SLO with a burn-rate alert

    SLO = {"quality_precision": 0.95, "p95_latency_ms": 1500, "cost_per_call": 0.02}

def evaluate_health(window):
    sli = {
        "quality_precision": sampled_eval(window),     # AI-specific
        "p95_latency_ms": p95(window.latencies),
        "cost_per_call": window.spend / window.calls,  # AI-specific
    }
    for k, target in SLO.items():
        worse = sli[k] < target if k == "quality_precision" else sli[k] > target
        if worse and burning_error_budget(k, window):
            page_oncall(k, sli[k], target)             # alert before full breach
    return sli
    07 · Analogies

    Four ways to picture AI-SRE

    An ICU monitor. It doesn't just check whether the patient is alive (uptime) — it tracks the vital signs that reveal deterioration early, and it alarms before a crisis, not after.

    Quality control on a production line: continuous sampling of finished goods, control charts that flag drift, and a documented response when a batch goes out of spec.

    Distributed tracing and SLOs — but the "error rate" includes a statistical quality score and a cost metric, and many failures can't be reproduced on demand.

    A control tower: it observes everything in the air, sets the rules for safe operation, coordinates the response to trouble, and reroutes around failures — without building or loading the planes.

    11 & 12 · Advantages & disadvantages

    Investing in AI-SRE: benefits and costs

    Advantages

    • Catches silent regressions before customers do.
    • Bounds cost with spend SLOs and alerts.
    • Faster recovery via runbooks and failover.
    • Continuous improvement through feedback to the SDLC.

    Disadvantages / realities

    • Observability cost — tracing and sampled evals add spend.
    • Quality SLIs are hard — defining "good" is itself work.
    • Alert tuning — noisy alerts cause fatigue.
    • On-call burden on humans must be managed.
    13 & 14 · Best practices & anti-patterns

    AI-SRE done well — and badly

    Best practices

    • Make quality and cost first-class SLIs, with SLOs and error budgets.
    • Trace full request context so unreproducible failures are debuggable.
    • Sample live traffic for evals to detect drift early.
    • Write runbooks; practise rollback and failover.
    • Blameless postmortems; feed root causes back to the SDLC.

    Anti-patterns

    • Uptime-only dashboards that miss quality collapse.
    • No tracing — failures can't be reproduced or inspected.
    • Ignoring cost until the monthly bill explodes.
    • Blurring the boundary — re-building/re-shipping here instead of detecting and handing back.

    16 · Architect notes

    Think like an architect

    Trade-off analysis

    Decide your quality SLI before you need it, and accept that it will be imperfect. A rough, cheap quality signal you actually watch (sampled LLM-as-judge, escalation rate) beats a perfect one you never built. The point of the SLO is to convert "the answers feel worse" into a paging condition — so bias toward a signal that is good enough to alert on today.

    Failure scenario

    A retrieval store degrades and starts returning stale documents. Uptime stays at 100%, latency is fine, so classic monitoring is silent — but refund quality slowly falls and customers escalate. Without a quality SLI and drift detection, the first "alert" is a wave of complaints days later.

    17 · Common mistakes

    Where teams go wrong

    • Measuring only uptime and latency on a probabilistic system.
    • No traces, so post-hoc debugging of odd failures is impossible.
    • Alert fatigue from noisy, poorly tuned thresholds.
    • No graceful degradation — the system goes dark when a provider fails.

    19 · Checklist

    Before you consider an AI system "operable"

    • Full-context tracing on every request.
    • Quality and cost SLIs defined, with SLOs and error budgets.
    • Alerts tuned to burn-rate, not single blips.
    • Runbooks for rollback, failover, and common incidents.

    21 · Summary

    Summary

    AI-SRE is the operate act of the lifecycle: keeping shipped AI healthy in production. It extends classic SRE with the signals AI demands — quality and cost as first-class SLIs beside latency and uptime — because the most dangerous AI failure is silent, not loud. It relies on full-context tracing to debug non-deterministic failures, on SLOs and error budgets to define "reliable enough," on alerting and runbook-driven incident response with sustainable on-call, and on drift detection, failover, and graceful degradation to stay trustworthy as the world shifts. It detects and contains — rolling back or failing over — and routes systemic problems back to the SDLC rather than rebuilding or re-shipping itself.

    With methodology (Ch 20), build-and-ship (Ch 21), and operations (Ch 22) in place, Exponential AI can run AI reliably at scale. That mastery of the present raises the natural next question: what is coming that will reshape all of it? Time to look outward, and far.
