    Chapter 02 · Learned Decisions

    Machine Learning: Exponential AI stops writing rules and starts learning them

    When the rules you need can't be written by hand, you learn them from data. This chapter follows Exponential AI as it trains its first models — to forecast demand and predict which customers are about to churn — and unpacks how a machine turns history into foresight.

    ~17 min read Level 1 → Level 3 Mermaid + ASCII diagrams Quiz included
    01 · Recap & Introduction

    Where Exponential AI left off

    Chapter 1 left Exponential AI staring at a wall: its most valuable decisions — how much to stock, which customers will leave — depend on patterns no human can write into a rule. The company had digitised a decade of transactions, tickets, and web logs. It had the raw material. What it lacked was a way to turn that history into decisions automatically.

    Machine learning is that way. Instead of a programmer specifying "if the customer hasn't bought in 60 days and opened no emails, they'll churn," Exponential AI shows an algorithm hundreds of thousands of past customers — who stayed, who left — and lets it discover the predictive pattern itself. This chapter is the pivot of the entire handbook: the move from writing logic to learning it.

    What you'll be able to do after this chapter

    Explain the three families of ML, tell classification from regression, describe the train/validate/test loop, reason about overfitting and the bias–variance trade-off, and see why ML still couldn't read a product photo — the gap Chapter 3 fills.

    02 · Why this technology exists

    Why the world invented machine learning

    ML exists because of the rule-writing bottleneck we hit in Chapter 1. For a vast class of problems, the mapping from input to correct answer is real but too complex, too fuzzy, or too fast-changing for a human to specify. Nobody can write the exact formula for "how many umbrellas will this store sell next week" — it depends on weather, season, price, local events, and trends in ways that interact nonlinearly.

    But we do have thousands of past weeks where we know both the conditions and the actual sales. ML flips the problem: rather than asking a human to derive the formula, it searches for a function that best reproduces the historical input→output pairs, then uses that function on new inputs. The insight is that data implicitly contains the rules; ML is the machinery for extracting them.

    There is a second, commercial reason ML exists: leverage. A hand-written rule requires a human to anticipate a situation before it happens. A learned model, given enough representative history, generalises to situations no one explicitly foresaw — and it does so at the speed and scale of software. For Exponential AI, that meant scoring every one of twelve million customers nightly for churn risk, a task that would have required an impossible army of analysts. ML converts a fixed data asset into a renewable stream of decisions, which is why it became the first pillar of the company's transformation rather than a lab curiosity.

    03 · Problem statement

    The problem, stated precisely

    Formally: given a dataset of examples (x, y) — where x is a feature vector (customer age, days since last order, email opens…) and y is the outcome (churned / stayed) — find a function f such that f(x) ≈ y, and, critically, such that it also works on new customers never seen during training. That last clause — generalisation — is the entire game.

    Two failure modes bracket the problem. Underfitting: f is too simple to capture the pattern (predict "nobody churns"). Overfitting: f memorises the training data — including its noise — and collapses on new data. The art of ML is finding the model that is exactly complex enough: the bias–variance sweet spot.

    Three levels of understanding

    One idea, three depths

    Level 1 · Simple

    Learning from examples

    Show a child enough photos of cats and dogs and they learn the difference without a definition. ML learns the same way — from labelled examples, not rules.

    Level 2 · Engineering

    Fit a function, minimise loss

    A model has parameters; a loss function measures how wrong its predictions are; an optimiser (e.g. gradient descent) nudges parameters to reduce loss over the training set — while you watch a held-out set to avoid overfitting.

    Level 3 · Architect

    Generalisation under distribution shift

    A production model is only as good as the stability of the data distribution it was trained on. Architects design for drift, monitoring, and retraining — because the world moves after you ship.

    04 · Historical evolution

    How machine learning matured

    Machine learning is older than most assume; what changed in the 2000s was not the core mathematics but the arrival of cheap storage, faster hardware, and — above all — vast digitised datasets. Exponential AI's own ML programme only became viable once its data warehouse existed. The timeline below shows how the field moved from theoretical promise to an industrial discipline.

    1950s–60s — Perceptrons & early learning

    The first trainable models; promising, then stalled by theoretical limits.

    1980s–90s — Statistical learning

    Decision trees, SVMs, and a rigorous theory of generalisation. ML becomes a science.

    2000s — Ensembles & big data

    Random forests and gradient boosting dominate tabular problems; cheap storage makes large datasets normal.

    2010s — Feature engineering era

    ML is productionised across industry; success hinges on humans hand-crafting good features — the very limit deep learning will remove in Chapter 3.

    05 · Internal architecture

    The anatomy of an ML system

    An ML system is a pipeline, not just a model. Raw data flows through feature engineering (turning messy records into numeric vectors), into a learning algorithm that fits parameters by minimising a loss function, evaluated on held-out data, and finally deployed behind an inference API that scores new inputs.

    The three families differ by what signal they learn from. Supervised learning uses labelled examples (churn: yes/no) — Exponential AI's bread and butter. Unsupervised learning finds structure without labels (customer segments). Reinforcement learning learns from reward signals through trial and error (dynamic pricing). Exponential AI's first two projects — churn and demand — are both supervised, the safest place to start.

    Within supervised learning, the choice of algorithm shapes everything downstream. Linear and logistic regression are transparent and fast but assume simple relationships. Decision trees capture nonlinearities and are easy to explain, but a single tree overfits. Ensembles — random forests and gradient-boosted trees — average or stack many weak trees into one strong, robust predictor, and for tabular enterprise data they remain the workhorse to this day. Exponential AI's team deliberately chose gradient boosting over a neural network for churn: on structured customer records it was more accurate, far cheaper to train, and easier to defend to auditors — a reminder that the newest tool is rarely the right one for tabular problems.

    06 · Step-by-step execution

    Training Exponential AI's churn model

    1. Frame. Define the label: a customer who makes no purchase in 90 days = churned.
    2. Assemble features. For each customer, compute recency, frequency, monetary value, email engagement, support tickets.
    3. Split. Partition history into train / validation / test sets (e.g. 70/15/15), keeping time order to avoid leakage.
    4. Train. Fit a gradient-boosted tree; the optimiser minimises log-loss on the training set.
    5. Validate & tune. Adjust hyperparameters against the validation set; stop when validation loss stops improving.
    6. Test & ship. Report metrics on the untouched test set, then serve predictions to the retention team — with monitoring for drift.

    The loop, as pseudocode

    model = GradientBoostingClassifier()
model.fit(X_train, y_train)                 # learn from labelled history

val_score = roc_auc(y_val, model.predict_proba(X_val))
# tune hyperparameters against val_score, then:
test_score = roc_auc(y_test, model.predict_proba(X_test))  # honest estimate

at_risk = model.predict_proba(X_live)[:, 1] > 0.6           # score new customers
    07 · Analogies

    Four ways to picture machine learning

    An apprentice sommelier who tastes thousands of labelled wines. Nobody hands them a rulebook; after enough examples they can place a new glass by region and vintage. ML is that palate, formalised.

    Exponential AI's best buyer, distilled. Their intuition for "this will sell" came from years of seeing outcomes. A demand model captures that intuition from the same history — and never retires.

    Curve-fitting as configuration. Instead of writing branches, you fit parameters so the function reproduces observed behaviour — like auto-tuning a controller from logs rather than hand-setting gains.

    A compiler from data to decisions. Training is compilation (slow, offline); inference is execution (fast, online). Drift is bit-rot: the compiled artefact degrades as the world diverges from its training data.

    11 & 12 · Advantages & disadvantages

    Strengths and costs

    Advantages

    • Scales to millions of decisions at near-zero marginal cost.
    • Consistent & objective — same input, same output.
    • Captures complex nonlinear patterns humans can't specify.
    • Improves with more data and periodic retraining.

    Disadvantages

    • Needs labelled data, which is costly to obtain.
    • Feature engineering is manual and expertise-heavy.
    • Drifts as the world changes; silent decay is real.
    • Can encode bias present in historical data.
    13 & 14 · Best practices & anti-patterns

    Doing ML well — and badly

    Best practices

    • Guard the test set — never tune on it; it's your only honest estimate.
    • Respect time order to prevent data leakage in temporal problems.
    • Start with a simple baseline before reaching for complex models.
    • Monitor in production for drift and retrain on a schedule.

    Anti-patterns

    • Data leakage — letting future information sneak into features.
    • Chasing accuracy on a skewed dataset (99% "no churn" looks great, predicts nothing).
    • Deploy-and-forget — ignoring drift until decisions rot.
    • Over-engineering a deep net where a tree wins on tabular data.
    15 · Interview questions

    Questions you should be able to answer

    A model that fits training noise and fails to generalise. Detect it when training performance is high but validation/test performance is much lower; combat it with more data, regularisation, or a simpler model.

    The validation set gets "used up" during tuning. An untouched test set gives an unbiased estimate of real-world performance.

    Bias = error from oversimplification (underfitting); variance = error from over-sensitivity to training data (overfitting). Total error is minimised at the balance point between them.

    Churn is rare, so predicting "nobody churns" scores high accuracy while being useless. Use precision/recall, F1, or ROC-AUC on the minority class.

    16 · Architect notes

    Think like an architect

    Trade-off analysis

    The dominant cost of classical ML isn't the model — it's features and labels. An architect budgets for the data pipeline and labelling operation first. This bottleneck is precisely what deep learning (Ch 3) and later foundation models attack by learning features automatically.

    Failure scenario

    Exponential AI's demand model, trained pre-pandemic, silently collapses when buying behaviour shifts overnight. No error is thrown — just quietly wrong forecasts. The lesson: models decay; monitoring and retraining are not optional.

    17 · Common mistakes

    Where teams go wrong

    • Leaking the label into features (e.g. using "cancellation date" to predict churn).
    • Ignoring class imbalance and trusting raw accuracy.
    • Tuning on the test set, then being shocked by production performance.
    • Assuming correlation is causation when acting on model outputs.

    19 · Checklist

    Before shipping an ML model

    • Problem framed with a clear label and success metric.
    • Train/validation/test split respects time order — no leakage.
    • A simple baseline beaten by the chosen model.
    • Metric appropriate for class balance (not raw accuracy).
    • Drift monitoring and a retraining schedule in place.
    • Fairness/bias reviewed on sensitive segments.

    21 · Summary

    Summary

    Machine learning replaced hand-written rules with functions learned from data. Exponential AI's first supervised models — churn classification and demand regression — turned a decade of history into forward-looking decisions at a scale no team of analysts could match. The craft lies in generalisation: splitting data honestly, balancing bias and variance, and monitoring for the drift that quietly erodes every deployed model.

    But classical ML carried a hidden dependency: it only worked once humans had hand-crafted good features. On tabular data that was fine. On raw pixels and raw language, it stalled — and Exponential AI's next ambition was exactly there.
